Introduction: Crime Analysis¶

This project aims to critically evaluate the claims made in Ross Kemp and the Armed Police [1] to determine their validity, using Street Level Crime data published by the UK Home Office. The analysis presented in this project will identify trends in crime over time in the United Kingdom, contributing to literature in the Crime and Big Data domain.

Claims to Evaluate¶

  1. Violent Crime is increasing.
  2. Crimes involving firearms are closely associated with drugs offences.
  3. There are more firearms incidents per head in Liverpool than anywhere else in the UK

Approach¶

  1. Process the crime dataset using Apache Spark on a cloud IaaS platform
  2. Filter and split the crimes into sets relevant to analyse each claim, combining with additional geographic and population data where necessary.
  3. Carry out analysis on the claims made.
    1. Task 1: Determine if violent crimes are increasing by fitting a SARIMA model, and inspecting the trend using time-series decomposition.
    2. Task 2: Use Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) to determine if firearm crime is correlated with drug offences.
    3. Task 2: Determine outlier cities for firearm crime and compare with Liverpool.
      • Perform clustering on cities to give more detailed view.
  4. Support analysis with visualisations.
  5. Critical discussion of approach.

# For some reason the first time i execute an adf_test on a fresh kernel it doesnt work first time
def adf_test(series, retries=3, delay=1):
    for attempt in range(retries):
        try:
            result = adfuller(series, maxlag=6)
            print("ADF Statistic:", result[0])
            print("p-value:", result[1])
            print("Critical Values:", result[4])
            return result[1]
        except np.linalg.LinAlgError:
            print(f"Attempt {attempt + 1} fail: SVD didnt converge")
            time.sleep(delay) 
    raise ValueError("ADF test failed completely.")

    F.count(F.when(F.col("is_firearms"), 1)).alias("firearm_incidents"),
    F.count(F.when(F.col("is_drugs"), 1)).alias("drug_incidents"),
    F.count(F.when(F.col("is_vc"), 1)).alias("vc_incidents")
)

# broadcast join on relevant crimes
incidents_lsoa_joined = F.broadcast(lsoa_df).join(
    aggregated_crime_df, "lsoa_code", "left"
).fillna(
    {"firearm_incidents": 0, "drug_incidents": 0, "vc_incidents": 0}
)

city_agg = (
    incidents_lsoa_joined
    .groupBy("city_clean")
    .agg(
        F.sum("firearm_incidents").alias("total_firearm_incidents"),
        F.sum("drug_incidents").alias("total_drug_incidents"),
        F.sum("vc_incidents").alias("total_vc_incidents"),
        F.sum("total_population").alias("total_population"),
        F.avg("density").alias("avg_density"),
    )
    .filter("total_population > 0")
    .withColumn("firearm_incidents_per_1000", (F.col("total_firearm_incidents") / F.col("total_population")) * 1000)
    .withColumn("drug_incidents_per_1000", (F.col("total_drug_incidents") / F.col("total_population")) * 1000)
    .withColumn("vc_incidents_per_1000", (F.col("total_vc_incidents") / F.col("total_population")) * 1000)
    .withColumn("is_liverpool", F.col("city_clean").rlike("(?i)Liverpool"))
    .withColumn("log_firearm", F.log1p("total_firearm_incidents"))
    .withColumn("log_drug", F.log1p("total_drug_incidents"))
    .withColumn("log_vc", F.log1p("total_vc_incidents"))
    .withColumn("log_density", F.log1p("avg_density"))

A SARIMA model will be trained on the stationary data vc_pd['diff'] using crimes up to 09/2018. The model will then forecast the following 24 months. Splitting this into two testing periods, to evaluate the models performance prior to and during COVID-19.

  • Kendall Test to test for upward trend in crime rates [18].
  • Visualisation 1 comparison between the real crime counts (log-transformed) and the SARIMA-generated forecasts.
  • Assess accuracy using Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE).

This approach aligns with existing literature that emphasises the importance of preventing data from the forecasted period being included in the training data to prevent overly optimistic model evaluations [17].

Claim 3: There are more gun crimes per person in Liverpool than other areas in the UK.¶

Outlier cities are plotted for firearm crime with their per capita rates and raw numbers, revealing if the claim is true or false. Following this Principal Compoent Analysis (PCA) is performed to reduce the dimensionality of the dataset. This is followed by K-Means clustering to group cities into clusters based on patterns in their features. A scatterplot of the PCA components with clustered highlighted will be produces, allowing for interpretation of Liverpool's position relative to other cities for firearm and related crime.

Claim 1: Violent Crime is increasing.¶

From the SARIMAX forecast plot, violent crime has been increasing since its low in 2011 to a peak in 2019, increasing by 158.27% om this time. The rate of increase outstrips population growth in England and Wales (The areas this data covers) which stood at 6.18% from 2011-2021. In addition this growth is not evenly distributed, with Wales growing significantly slower [4].

Overview of Approach¶

  1. Split data for training prior to 09/2018.
  2. Use the Kendall Tau test to determine if there is a statistically significant trend in violent crime increase over time in the training data [22].
  3. Apply SARIMA forecast violent crime rates for the 24 months following 09/2018.
  4. Evaluate the accuracy of the forecast in two parts pre and post COVID-19.
  5. Testing Periods:
    • Pre-COVID (Oct 2018 Sep 2019): Evaluates the model during a stable period.
    • COVID-19 Period (Oct 2019 Sep 2020): Assesses the model's handling of disruptions.

SARIMA Model Results¶

SARIMA[12] was to capture monthly seasonality and forecasting ability.

  • 80/20 Train/Test split
  • Differencing was applied to remove trends and achieve stationarity tested using the Augmented-Dickey Fuller Test.
  • Seasonal decomposition revealed significant periodic components, supporting the use of seasonal parameters.
  • Without exogenous variables, the models ability to account for external shocks is limited. Future iterations would explore using House Price Index data and other factors.
  • This modelling does not account for the different rates of growth in different regions.

6) Analysis of the appropriateness of the solution¶

The proposed solution leverages Apache Spark for computationally intensive tasks and converting to Pandas DataFrames for analysis. This ensuring efficient processing of big data. Integration ofpopulation data enabled per capita analysis reducing the skew of larger population density [6]. SARIMA implementation could be improved by using exogoneous variables. OLS regression then explored the strength and interaction of additional variables, giving a comprehensive statistical foundation. In addition VIF analysis addressed multicollinearity, enhancing the reliability of insights.

  1. Simplistic SARIMA implementation due to the lack of exogenous variables, means the SARIMA models forecasts may overlook critical external factors such as socioeconomic changes.
    • Furthermore outlier years 2020-2021 which have vastly different violent crime rates likely due to COVID-19 damage the models forecasting ability.
  2. Without integrating further geographical data, such as the urban / rural environments may miss nuances in crime patterns [16].
    • In addition, further analysis of geography could be done by incorporating longitude and latitude variables for further spatial analysis.
  3. The population data in LSOA_pop_v2.csv is limited to 2011, skewing the per capita calculations.

Limitations¶

  1. A lack of comprehensive socio-economic data such as income by region and population data over time means that critical factors that may influence crime hotspots remains unexplored such as geographic features.
  2. The LSOA_pop_v2 dataset exhibits significant diversity across LSOA codes in both population and diversity, showing the difficulty in drawing conclusions for England and Wales generally.
  3. There could be an argument for surrounding areas of major cities eg. Liverpool and the City of London (outliers in this analysis) to be counted in their firearm incident rate, however this remains unexplored in this research.
  4. Some research indicates some groups are more likely to report crimes in their area, possibly skewing the data analysed in this report. [7]

Despite its limitations, the designed solution effectively analysed the three claims from providing quantitative insights. The combination of statistical models and use of machine learning to produce clear visualisations offers a data-driven approach to fact checking.

7) Evaluation and Conclusion¶

Claim 1: Violent Crime is Increasing¶

SARIMA modeling revealed a clear upward trend in violent crime from 2011 to 2019, supported by a statistically significant Kendall Tau test ( = 0.7573, p < 0.01). Data stationarity was ensured using differencing and validated with the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test.

  • Violent crime increased by 158.27% between 2011 and 2019, outpacing population growth over this time (6.18% across England and Wales).
  • Forecasting projected violent crime to continue to increase in 2022, following a decrease during COVID-19 (20202021).

The SARIMA model effectively captured seasonality and long-term trends but lacked exogenous variables to account for external shocks like COVID-19 and economic factors.

Claim 2: Firearm Crime and Drug Crime are Closely Associated¶

  • Pearson Correlation confirmed a significant relationship between firearm and drug crimes.
  • OLS Regression further explored this relationship, incorporating population density and violent crime as predictors.
  1. Strong correlations were observed between firearm and drug crimes, with violent crime identified as a key factor.
  2. Adjusted R values of 81.6% (firearm model) and 86.7% (drug model) indicated robust explanatory power. The models effectively quantified the association but highlighted multicollinearity issues, suggesting the need for interaction terms or an alternative approach.

Claim 3: Liverpool Has the Highest Firearm Crime per Capita¶

  • Per capita firearm rates were calculated using LSOA population data.
  • Outlier analysis and K-Means clustering (with PCA) grouped cities based on crime patterns.
  1. Liverpool had the highest total firearm incidents, but per capita rates placed it among, high-crime cities.
  2. London boroughs, such as the City of London and Westminister are prominent in firearm rate outliers.
  3. Clustering confirmed Liverpool's similarity to other high-crime cities, supporting the claim as mostly false.

The approach provided detailed insights into crime distribution but lacked granular demographic or geographic context for a deeper understanding. No exploration of Liverpool firearm crime over time is carried out.

Conclusion¶

The project critically evaluated the claims made in Ross Kemp and the Armed Police using robust, data-driven methods:

Claim 1: Violent Crime is Increasing: True

  • Violent crime consistently increased from 2011 to 2019, with SARIMA forecasting steayd increase after the COVID-19 disruption.
  • A clear upward trend in violent crime exists, outstripping population growth.
  • The analysis in this report provides robust evidence to support this claim.
  • Forecasting added value to this assessment by projecting future trends in crime to stabilise in the year 2022.

Claim 2: Firearm and Drug Crime are Associated: True Statistical analysis confirmed a strong association, however violent crime is more closely correlated. Claim 3: Liverpool Has the Highest Firearm Crime per Capita: Mostly False While Liverpool has the highest total firearm incidents, its per capita rate is not the highest among the regions evaluated in this work.

  • A possible explanation for the claim is the historical and cultural stereotyping associated with Liverpool with some research indicating its association with criminality, untrustworthiness, working-class identity and drugs [3], [10], [11]. Suggesting that the perception of crime in Liverpool is influenced by regional discrimination rather than crime data.
  • London boroughs are significant outliers in firearm crime statistics. ### Misinformation Misinformation via social media or mainstream media has become a growing area of concern, especially post pandemic in the U.K [24],and with low confidence in media institutions [25] ensuring transparency and independently validatable claims is critical to maintaining trust, especially with the role of mainstream media in the spreading of COVID-19 related misinformation [26].
